Academic Skills Crash Course (October 2019)

The slides for the Academic Skills Crash Course I ran in October of 2019 are linked below. Some of the slides have been changed a little bit from the version I used during the class. If you have questions, feel free to email me at andersc@hhu.de.

Slides: anderson-skills.pdf

The slides included lots of links to resources available online. These are the links.

Day 1: Topic statements, finding topics, finding sources, reading more efficiently

Topics:

  • Subjects versus topics
  • Narrowing your topic
  • Library resources for finding sources
  • Reading for a goal
  • Structure of linguistics papers (theoretical versus experimental)
  • Strategies in reading

Day 2: Arguments, writing like a linguist, citation style

Topics:

  • Types of arguments
  • Claims, evidence, and assumptions
  • Structuring linguistic arguments
  • Defending an argument
  • General conventions in writing
  • Conventions in linguistics (examples, glossing)

Day 3: Citations, plagiarism, and pedagogy

Topics:

  • When and what to cite
  • Citation styles
  • Why plagiarism happens
  • Avoiding plagiarism
  • Tips on teaching
  • Ways of getting people to talk

  • In-text citation exercise (external link)

External (Non-HHU) Resources

Resources developed by other universities.
  • The University of California at Berkeley has a resources page for their graduate student instructors which will be very useful for you. (recommended)

  • Tips for linguistics students by Daniel Büring (Universität Wien)
  • How to write an essay in linguistics (WayBack Machine link, since the original is broken) (recommended)
  • plagiarism.org has discussion of what counts and doesn't count as plagiarism.
  • Exercise on plagiarism
  • More exercises and information about plagiarism from Florida State University
  • Slides on how to paraphrase
  • Example numbering in Microsoft Word.
  • An exercise on in-text citations.
  • The Three Things exercise for getting more out of talks. You could use this as an exercise for papers, too.
  • How to make an annotated bibliography
  • Pomodoro technique for managing your time
  • Developing a topic and narrowing a research question
  • Handbook for Teaching Assistants (Queen's University, Ontario) (recommended)

Books

There are a couple books I also referenced while building this class, or were suggested to me, but there are many guides for academic writing (and academic skills) you can find.
  • Surviving Linguistics, by Monica McCauley (recommended)
  • The Craft of Research (4th Ed.), Wayne C. Booth, Gregory G. Colomb, Joseph M. Williams, Joseph Bizup, and William T. Fitzgerald (recommended)
  • The Unwritten Rules of PhD Research, by Gordon Rugg and Marian Petre
  • Lesen und Schreiben, by Otto Kruse (available from the university library as an ebook)
